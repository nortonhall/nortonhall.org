On the Impossibility of Non-Existence

There is a particular kind of circle people find themselves caught in when they try to think seriously about nothing. Not a simple confusion, but a looping. The argument turns back on itself. Definitions multiply. Objections cancel one another out. People say the problem is too abstract, or that it exposes the limits of human thought, or that language simply breaks down here.

And then the discussion stalls.

This reaction is so common that it is often taken as evidence that the question itself is misguided, or unanswerable in principle. But it may be pointing to something more specific. It may be telling us that we are trying to approach the question in the wrong way.

I believe this comes from a tendency that is easy to overlook. You are probably already doing it now. As you read, you are placing this, matching it to something familiar, however loosely. You do not leave it undefined. You bring it into relation with what you already know, and in doing so, you give it a shape it can be handled through.

This is natural, and in most cases it is useful. It allows us to move quickly, to orient ourselves, to make sense of what we encounter.

But here, it matters, because what is being considered is unusually difficult to think about properly, and when something resists clear thought, this tendency does not fall away. It becomes more active. What cannot be easily held is quietly reshaped into something that can be.

Consider how the word "nothing" actually functions.

When we say there is nothing in the cupboard, we mean it is empty of what we were looking for. When we say nothing happened, we mean nothing of note occurred. When we say there is nothing left, we mean nothing remains of a certain kind. In each case, "nothing" marks an absence within a situation that is already there. It depends upon that situation, and makes sense only against it.

It never refers to the absence of the situation itself.

But when people ask whether there could have been nothing at all, they mean something stronger. No objects, no space, no time, no relations, no conditions, no distinctions. No background. No context. Not an empty situation, but no situation at all.

And yet, as soon as we try to think about that, something shifts. We move, often without noticing, between two different uses of the word "nothing". On the one hand, it names an absence within a situation. On the other, it is taken to mean non-existence as such, the absence of any situation at all.

These are not the same.

But they are easily conflated. The familiar use gives us a foothold, and we carry its structure over into what was supposed to lack all structure. What made sense as a local absence is extended, without warrant, to the absence of everything.

This is the same tendency as before. What cannot be placed is quietly placed anyway. What cannot be described is approached through forms of description that already assume structure.

Each attempt to make sense of nothing adds structure in order to explain the absence of structure. One person insists that nothing could not have laws. Another replies that without laws nothing would be unstable. Someone else points out that instability already presupposes time. The argument does not settle, because it has been forced into a form it cannot sustain.

The difficulty is not simply that nothing is hard to think about. It is that every attempt to think it draws upon resources that already contradict it.

This becomes clearer if we follow one of the most common intuitions. We tend to imagine that everything could be removed, layer by layer, until nothing remains, as though non-existence were what is left when enough has been destroyed. Reality is pictured as something that could, in principle, be taken away piece by piece.

But destruction always occurs within what is already the case. Something changes, something is lost, but something remains. Buildings collapse into rubble, objects burn into ash, information is erased but leaves traces. Even the most extreme forms of annihilation transform what exists rather than remove being altogether.

Even in our most advanced physical theories, nothing is ever reduced to non-existence as such. It is transformed, redistributed, or converted, but it remains part of the overall situation.

The situation alters, but it does not disappear.

There is no point at which the situation itself gives way to non-existence.

Destruction does not lead to non-existence, but to a different state of what is the case. However far the process is imagined to go, it never arrives at what is being aimed at. The idea of "nothing" is not approached asymptomatically. It is missed entirely.

And yet the thought persists. If it cannot be reached in this way, perhaps it was there to begin with. Perhaps something came from nothing.

But notice what this requires. For something to come from nothing, nothing must in some way allow it, permit it, or give rise to it. It must have a character, however minimal. A capacity. A condition under which such a transition could occur. It must, in some sense, be the kind of thing from which something could emerge.

But that is already not nothing.

To say that something comes from nothing is to give nothing a role, a function, a way of being. It is to treat it as though it could do something, or support something, or stand in some relation to what follows.

It is to import, quietly, exactly what was meant to be absent.

If this is set aside, what remains is often expressed more cautiously. Perhaps nothing was possible in principle. Perhaps there did not need to be anything at all. This shifts the claim, but does not escape the problem. Because it still assumes that "nothing" names a genuine alternative that could have obtained.

But at this point something has become clear. We cannot specify nothing without turning it into something. We cannot reach it by removal. We cannot use it in explanation without assigning it structure. It does not appear in action, in observation, or in any model that can be made coherent.

So the comparison is not between two equally formed possibilities.

On one side, there is everything we encounter, think, and do, all of which operates only within what is already the case. On the other, an idea sustained by a conflation: an absence within a situation taken as though it were the absence of the situation itself, and which cannot be specified without contradiction, cannot be encountered, and cannot be made to hold without being altered into something else.

Which of these should be treated as more likely?

The point is not that we have proven, in some final sense, that something must exist. It is that the alternative never stabilises. It does not present itself as a genuine option alongside what is the case, but collapses whenever it is examined closely enough to be used.

"Nothing" does not stand alongside "something is the case" as a competing possibility. It marks the point at which alternatives fall apart.

Nothing that has been said here is intended to be novel. These are observations that have likely been made, in one form or another, throughout the history of thought. The difficulty is not that they are unknown, but that they are rarely held in view long enough for their consequences to be taken seriously.

If non-existence was never available, then something must obtain. And if something not only is the case, but must be the case, then its consequences do not remain confined to the question of non-existence.

They cascade, and in doing so, begin to dissolve questions that have long appeared beyond the reach of human thought.
